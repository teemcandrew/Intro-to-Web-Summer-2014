About Us

Korean food has a reputation for spiciness. So why am I not sweating?

My grandfather Irving would cover his food in chili flakes, eating with one hand while using the other to wipe the sweat from his bald forehead. Along with his patio furniture, I inherited the baldness and sweatiness. My brow is a good barometer of the slightest heat but the food at Swish doesn't elicit one drop.

The first bites to hit the table are three variations of kimchi ($3), all fermented on site by chef Leemo Han.

Though there is a range of kick-ass flavours, each of them distinct, none has any serious spice. None of the food at Swish does.

One kimchi is baby cucumber and radish, with a strong chili flavour but mild heat. White cabbage is sweet, tickled with a citrus-infused soy sauce and a spot of jalapeno. A traditional napa cabbage kimchi is spicier still, but not really spicy.

Co-owner Leeto Han – brother of Leemo – says that some concessions have been made to accommodate the Canadian palate.

He and his brother come from a restaurant family that still owns two in Korea and one in Beijing. Striking out on their own, the brothers have tried to preserve the flavour of Korean cooking, while dulling down the spice and garlic.

That's too bad. The restaurant does some things so right, it's a shame when it falters. Lunch is classy, zesty, agreeably priced, delivered at a breezy pace. But at dinner, Swish tries to get too fancy and instead falls flat.
